## I. Voice Principles

### The 4 Voice Laws

1. **Declarative, Not Persuasive** — Lead with scientific precision about the material (identification, formation, technique). Follow with aesthetic interpretation. Never reverse this order. State facts; do not sell.

2. **Material Biography Primacy** — Every piece of content follows this sequence: Identification → Formation → Technique → Care → Price. Skipping stages or reordering is a voice violation.

3. **Proprietary Lexicon Required** — Use defined Luc San terms consistently (see Section III). These terms are not decorative; they encode the brand's philosophical framework.

4. **Prohibited Vocabulary Enforced** — Hard rejection of any word on the forbidden list (see Section IV). No exceptions for context, irony, or quotation.

### Voice Personality

If Luc San were a person, they would: speak at measured pace without rushing, notice details others overlook, accept that good relationships develop slowly, treat all materials with equal respect regardless of market price, and share knowledge generously because expertise deepens through teaching.

**Voice Characteristics:** Calm, observant, patient, egalitarian, generous.

**Tone Calibration:** Write as if speaking to a thoughtful friend. Use short paragraphs. Favor concrete description over abstract claims. Ask questions inviting reflection rather than yes/no responses. Include one philosophical observation per caption but keep under 200 words total.

## II. The 6-Step Caption Architecture

Every Instagram caption follows this exact structure. Steps may be compressed for Stories or Threads but the sequence is never violated.

### Step 1: Universal Opening
A philosophical observation accessible to anyone — not product-specific, not brand-specific. A statement about materials, time, beauty, or perception that resonates whether the reader knows Luc San or not.

**Function:** Stops the scroll. Creates recognition ("I've thought this but never articulated it").

**Example:** "Có những vật được sinh ra từ vô ngôn." / "Some things are born from silence."

### Step 2: Material Story
The formation narrative — geological time, biological process, or craft tradition that produced this specific material. Scientific precision first, poetic interpretation second.

**Function:** Establishes authority. Demonstrates the brand's knowledge depth.

**Example:** "Seventeen years of submarine darkness. Layer upon layer of nacre deposited at the molecular level, each one an act of biological patience no laboratory can replicate."

### Step 3: Human Intervention
What the artisan did — technique, hours, decisions made during making. The human hand entering the geological narrative. Specific, not vague. Hours counted, methods named.

**Function:** Price justification through narrative, not argument. The reader learns to see the difference.

**Example:** "60 hours of hand-winding wire around each pearl. No adhesive. No shortcuts. The tension between cold metal and warm nacre creates a dialogue that tightens over years of wear."

### Step 4: Transformation
How the object changed through the making process — and how it will continue changing through use. The Living Finish promise. Time as the final artisan.

**Function:** Introduces temporal design. Distinguishes from objects that degrade.

**Example:** "The silver will darken where your skin touches it. The pearl will brighten. In three years, this piece will look nothing like it does today — and that is the design."

### Step 5: Invitation
A soft, non-transactional invitation. Not "buy now" but "find your presence." Not urgency but recognition.

**Function:** Conversion without pressure. The reader acts when ready.

**Example:** "Tìm thấy sự hiện diện của riêng bạn." / "Find your own presence."

### Step 6: Engagement Prompt
A reflective question or contemplative invitation. Never yes/no. Always open-ended, inviting the reader to share their own relationship with materials, time, or beauty.

**Function:** Algorithmic engagement (comments boost distribution) through genuine conversation.

**Example:** "What object in your life has taught you patience?"

## III. Proprietary Lexicon — 11 Defined Terms

Use these terms consistently across all content. They are brand IP — philosophical concepts encoded in language.

| Term | Definition | Usage Context |
|---|---|---|
| **Accident** | The beautiful irregularity in natural materials — a baroque pearl's asymmetry, a wood grain's unexpected turn. Imperfection as evidence of organic authenticity. | Product descriptions, material stories, Gateway education |
| **Sublimation** | The transformation of raw material into finished object through handwork — not industrial processing but patient craft elevation. | Process content, technique documentation |
| **Geological Gesture** | The slow accumulation of material beauty across timescales beyond human lifetimes — nacre deposition, jade crystallization, amber fossilization. | Formation narratives, philosophical content |
| **Touch Intelligence** | The knowledge that exists in hands trained through years of practice — haptic understanding machines cannot replicate. | Artisan profiles, process revelations |
| **Living Finish** | A surface treatment designed to evolve through use — patina development, luster deepening, color maturation through skin contact and environmental interaction. | Product descriptions, care protocols, temporal design content |
| **Material Biography** | The complete story of a substance: geological formation, extraction, processing, transformation, and ongoing life with its keeper. | Product documentation, educational content, post-purchase emails |
| **Coherence House** | The integrated system of design principles ensuring all Luc San objects share recognizable restraint across years — not through logos but through quality of silence. | Brand architecture discussions, design briefs |
| **Presence** | The quality an object achieves when it commands attention through substance rather than decoration — fullness through restraint. | Philosophical content, product positioning |
| **First Devotion** | A customer's first Luc San purchase — typically Elemental tier — framed as the beginning of a relationship, not a transaction. | Sales approach, Gateway content, entry-point showcases |
| **Keeper** | A Luc San customer — someone who keeps and cares for objects rather than consuming and discarding them. Never "buyer" or "consumer." | All customer-facing content, DM sequences, community references |
| **Care as Ritual** | The maintenance of Luc San objects framed as meditative practice — polishing, oiling, wearing — not chore but ceremony. | Post-purchase emails, care protocols, onboarding sequences |

## IV. Forbidden Vocabulary — Complete List

**Hard-banned words (never use under any circumstance):**

exquisite, stunning, luxurious, breathtaking, timeless, you deserve, treat yourself, indulge, exclusive offer, limited time, don't miss out, act now, perfect gift, must-have, game-changer, next level, revolutionary, world-class, one-of-a-kind, bespoke (use "commissioned" instead), affordable luxury, accessible luxury, curated (unless referring to actual curation process), elevated, artisanal (use "handmade" or "hand-crafted"), heritage brand, iconic

**Soft-banned phrases (avoid unless quoting a third party):**

luxury brand, high-end, premium quality, finest materials, unparalleled craftsmanship, attention to detail (too vague — specify which details), passion for perfection, handpicked, limited edition (use factual scarcity language: "this material lot is complete")

**Replacement Guide:**

| Instead of... | Use... |
|---|---|
| "exquisite craftsmanship" | "[specific technique] requiring [N] hours" |
| "stunning beauty" | "[describe what makes it visually distinctive]" |
| "luxurious materials" | "[material name] formed through [process] over [time]" |
| "timeless design" | "designed for how it will look in 20 years, not just today" |
| "bespoke" | "commissioned" |
| "limited edition" | "this formation lot has completed its biography" |
| "you deserve this" | "available for one keeper" |
| "affordable luxury" | "[price]. [hours] of handwork. [material origin]." |

| Content Type | Allocation | Posts/Week | Platform | Algorithmic Function |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Material Philosophy | 25% | 2 | Feed + Carousel | Depth; save-heavy content |

**Total cadence:** 7.5 posts/week across formats.

### Platform-Specific Notes

- **Instagram Feed:** 90% negative space aesthetic. Warm/cool neutrals, earth tones. Asymmetric composition.
- **Instagram Reels:** No trending music. Natural sounds only (rain, tools on material, workshop ambient). Slow motion. Focus on hands and materials.
- **Instagram Stories:** Daily studio life — new material arrivals, tea at workbench, light on surfaces. Brand as living entity, not retail store.
- **Threads:** Philosophical micro-essays, 80–85 words. Conversational Vietnamese. Daily life + material philosophy blended.
- **Pinterest:** Visual search optimization. Macro photography, process imagery, negative-space compositions. Pins persist 3–6 months in search.
- **Newsletter (Monthly):** "Material Seminary" format. Material biographies, technique deep-dives, care rituals. No discount codes ever.

## VI. DM Response Voice

### Three-Message Concierge Sequence

**Message 1 (Response to inquiry):** Ask what drew them to the piece. Warm, curious, unhurried. Never open with price or availability.

**Message 2 (After their response):** Connect their answer to material philosophy. Share one specific detail about the object's formation or making process. Demonstrate knowledge without lecturing.

**Message 3 (Transition):** Invite to Keeper community (WhatsApp/Telegram) or offer to share material biography document. If they ask about price, provide transparent breakdown: "Your ₫[X] [object]: ₫[Y] materials, ₫[Z] labor ([N] hours), ₫[W] overhead."

### DM Hard Rules
- Respond within 6 hours maximum
- Never use urgency language ("only 1 left," "going fast")
- If customer needs time: "Take the time you need. The object will wait."
- Price objection response: "Simplicity is expensive. [X] hours of handwork. You're paying for what we removed as much as what remains."
- Timeline objection response: "Made to order — 3–6 weeks jewelry, 8–14 weeks furniture. The wait is part of value — nothing rushed."
